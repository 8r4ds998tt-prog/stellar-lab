You explored cells in the Inside the Cell mission. The test also wants the three ways materials move in and out of cells.

Cell types & parts

Animal & plant cells are eukaryotic (DNA in a nucleus); bacteria are prokaryotic (DNA as a loop, no nucleus). Plant extras: cell wall, chloroplasts, vacuole.

Specialised cells

Structure fits function — red blood cells (no nucleus, more O₂), nerve cells (long), sperm (tail + mitochondria), root hair (big surface area).

Diffusion & osmosis

Diffusion: particles spread high→low concentration (passive). Osmosis: water moving across a partially permeable membrane, dilute→concentrated.

Active transport

Moves particles low→high concentration (against the gradient) using energy from respiration — e.g. root hair cells absorbing mineral ions.

Blood & vessels

Blood = red cells (O₂), white cells (defence), platelets (clotting), plasma (transport). Artery away from heart (high pressure); vein back (valves); capillary one cell thick.

Gas exchange

In the alveoli: O₂ diffuses into the blood, CO₂ out. Big surface area, moist, thin walls, good blood supply.

Inside the atom — and how we separate mixtures. Two missions back this up.

Sub-atomic particles

Proton +1 (mass 1), neutron 0 (mass 1), electron −1 (~0). Atomic number = protons. Shells fill 2, 8, 8.

Isotopes

Same element, different number of neutrons (so different mass number). Relative atomic mass is the average across all isotopes.

Separating mixtures

Filtration (insoluble solid + liquid), crystallisation & simple distillation (solute from solvent), fractional distillation (liquids), chromatography.

Atom models

Dalton → plum pudding → alpha scattering → nuclear (Bohr) shells. Alpha scattering showed the atom is mostly empty space with a tiny dense nucleus.

How the table is built, and the group trends you must be able to explain.

Building the table

Modern table is ordered by atomic number and grouped by electrons in the outer shell. Mendeleev left gaps for undiscovered elements.

Group 1 — alkali metals

One outer electron. More reactive down the group — the outer electron is further out and more shielded, so lost more easily.

Group 7 — halogens

Seven outer electrons. Less reactive down the group — harder to gain an electron when the outer shell is further from the nucleus.

Group 0 — noble gases

Full outer shells, so inert (unreactive). Boiling points increase down the group.

🔒 GCSE stretch · bond energies

Breaking bonds takes in energy (endothermic); making bonds releases energy (exothermic).

Overall energy change = energy to break bonds − energy to make bonds. A positive answer = endothermic; negative = exothermic.

How quickly energy moves by heating — and how we slow it down.

Thermal conductivity

How fast a material conducts heat. Test it by timing how fast wax melts on rods of different metals. Low conductivity = good insulator.

Insulating buildings

Reduce heat loss with thick walls, loft & cavity-wall insulation, double glazing, draught excluders, foil behind radiators.

Specific heat capacity

The energy to raise 1 kg of a substance by 1 °C. Materials with a high value heat up and cool down slowly.

Rate of heat loss depends on

The material's conductivity, the temperature difference across it, and its thickness.

🔒 GCSE stretch · the equation

Specific heat capacity: ΔE = m c Δθ — energy = mass × specific heat capacity × temperature change.

Changing state

A physical change — mass is conserved because no particles are lost. The flat part of a heating curve is a change of state (latent heat).

Density & gas pressure

Density ρ = m / V. Heating a sealed gas raises its pressure — particles hit the walls more often and harder.

🔒 GCSE stretch · latent heat

Latent heat: E = m L — energy to change the state of mass m with no temperature change.
