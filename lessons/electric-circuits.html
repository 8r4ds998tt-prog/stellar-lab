Section 01 // Core Concept

Current, voltage & resistance

A circuit needs a complete loop and an energy source (a cell or battery). Three quantities describe what's happening inside it.

Current (I)

The flow of electric charge, measured in amps (A) with an ammeter connected in series.

Potential difference (V)

The "push" from the battery, measured in volts (V) with a voltmeter connected across a component (in parallel).

Resistance (R)

How much a component opposes the current, measured in ohms (Ω). More resistance → smaller current.

The loop rule

Break the loop anywhere — open a switch — and the current stops everywhere. Charge can only flow in a complete circuit.

Section 03 // Two Ways To Wire

Series vs parallel

➖ Series

One single loop. The same current flows through every component, and the battery's voltage is shared between the bulbs — so they're dimmer. Remove one bulb and they all go out.

⫴ Parallel

Separate branches. Each bulb gets the full voltage, so they're brighter, and the current splits between branches. Remove one bulb and the others stay lit — like the lights in a house.

Section 04 // Encrypted: GCSE Stretch

Advanced data

🔒 Ohm's law & the circuit rules
Ohm's law: V = I × R. Rearrange for current (I = V ÷ R) or resistance (R = V ÷ I). This is what the Circuit Lab is calculating.
Series rules: current is the same everywhere; the voltages across each component add up to the battery voltage; resistances add (R_total = R₁ + R₂).
Parallel rules: each branch has the full battery voltage; the branch currents add up to the total current; adding branches lowers the total resistance.

Worked example: a 6 V battery with a single 3 Ω bulb gives I = V ÷ R = 6 ÷ 3 = 2 A. Put two 3 Ω bulbs in series and R_total = 6 Ω, so I = 1 A — half as much, so they're dimmer.
