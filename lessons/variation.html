Section 01 // Core Concept

The same... but different

Members of the same species share the big features but differ in the details. These differences are called variation, and they come in two patterns from two main sources.

Continuous variation

A smooth range with every value in between — like height or hand span. Plotted, it makes a bell-shaped curve.

Discontinuous variation

A few distinct categories with no in-betweens — like blood group or tongue rolling. Plotted, it makes a few separate bars.

Genetic causes

Differences you inherit in your genes — eye colour, blood group, natural hair colour. You're born with them.

Environmental causes

Differences caused by your surroundings and life — a scar, a language you speak, a suntan. Many traits (like height) are caused by both.

Section 03 // Where It Comes From

Why we're all different

Most variation begins at the moment a new life is made — when genes from two parents are shuffled together.

🧬

Two parents, one mix

In sexual reproduction a sperm cell and an egg cell (the gametes) join at fertilisation. The offspring gets half its genes from each parent — a brand-new combination.

👯

Identical twins

They share the same genes, yet still differ in scars, fitness or tastes. That extra difference must come from the environment.

🌍

Variation = survival

Because individuals vary, some are better suited to their surroundings. That variation is the raw material that lets a species adapt and evolve.

⚡

Mutations

Random changes to genes (mutations) create brand-new variation that never existed in the parents — most do nothing, a few are harmful, a rare one is useful.

Section 04 // Encrypted: GCSE Stretch

Advanced data

🔒 Chromosomes, gametes & sex cells
Chromosomes: your genes are carried on 23 pairs of chromosomes — 46 in total — inside the nucleus of nearly every cell.
Gametes (sperm and egg) are made by a special division called meiosis and carry only 23 chromosomes each — half the normal number.
Fertilisation joins the two gametes, restoring 46 chromosomes and combining genes from both parents — the source of genetic variation.

Sex chromosomes: one pair decides biological sex — XX (female) or XY (male).

Continuous traits are usually controlled by many genes together plus the environment, which is why they blend into a smooth range. Discontinuous traits are often controlled by just one or two genes.
