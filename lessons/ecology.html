Section 01 // Core Concept

Everything is connected

An ecosystem is all the living things in an area plus their non-living surroundings. Inside it, organisms feed on one another and rely on each other to survive — this is interdependence.

The key words

Habitat = where something lives. Population = all of one species there. Community = all the populations together. Ecosystem = the community plus its physical environment.

Producers & consumers

Producers (plants, algae) make their own food using sunlight. Consumers eat other living things. A food chain always starts with a producer.

Biotic vs abiotic

Biotic factors are living (predators, food, disease). Abiotic factors are non-living (light, temperature, water, minerals). Both decide what survives where.

Where the energy starts

Nearly all energy in an ecosystem comes from the Sun. Plants trap a tiny bit of it by photosynthesis — and that energy then passes along every food chain.

Trophic level

Select any organism in the pyramid to see its job and how much energy it holds.

The 10% rule: only ~10% of the energy at one level is passed on. That is why food chains are short — there is rarely enough energy left for a fifth link.
Section 03 // In The Wild

Balancing act

Because everything is linked, a change to one species ripples through the whole web.

🦊🐇

Predator & prey

More rabbits → more food for foxes → fox numbers rise → more rabbits eaten → rabbit numbers fall. The two cycle up and down together.

🕸️

Food webs

Real ecosystems are food webs — many chains linked. Remove one species and everything that ate it, or that it ate, is affected.

🍄

Decomposers

Fungi and bacteria break down dead plants and animals, returning nutrients to the soil so producers can grow again.

🌡️

Adapt or move

Organisms are suited to their habitat — a polar bear's fat and fur, a cactus's water store. Change the abiotic factors and some species can no longer survive.

Section 04 // Encrypted: GCSE Stretch

Advanced data

🔒 Trophic levels, efficiency & sampling
Trophic levels are numbered: producers are level 1, herbivores (primary consumers) level 2, then 3, 4… Apex predators sit at the top with no predators of their own.
Why energy is lost: most is used in respiration (movement and keeping warm) and escapes as heat; more is lost in waste (faeces, urine) and in uneaten parts (bones, roots).
Efficiency of transfer = (energy passed on ÷ energy available) × 100 — usually only about 10%.

Pyramids of biomass show the mass of living material at each level. They are always pyramid-shaped (wide producers, narrow top) because so much energy is lost going up.

Sampling: ecologists estimate populations using a quadrat (a square frame placed at random) to count plants, or a transect line to see how a community changes across an area.
