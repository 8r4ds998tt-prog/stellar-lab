THE RIGHT
TRIANGLE

One of the oldest rules in maths: in any right-angled triangle, the squares on the two short sides add up to the square on the longest. Build one and see.

Begin Briefing▾
Section 01 // Core Concept

Squares on the sides

In a right-angled triangle, the longest side — opposite the right angle — is the hypotenuse. Pythagoras' theorem links all three sides:

a² + b² = c²

The hypotenuse (c)

Always the longest side, and always opposite the right angle. It's the c in a² + b² = c².

Why squares?

Build a square on each side. The two smaller squares' areas (a² and b²) together exactly equal the big square's area (c²).

Find the hypotenuse

If you know both short sides: c = √(a² + b²). Square them, add, then square-root.

Right angles only

This rule works only for right-angled triangles — the little square in the corner is your signal to use it.

Section 03 // Two Jobs

Which side are you finding?

📐

Finding the hypotenuse

You know the two short sides → add the squares, then square-root: c = √(a² + b²). The answer is the longest side.

🧩

Finding a shorter side

You know the hypotenuse and one short side → subtract: a = √(c² − b²). Take the hypotenuse square away first.

🪜

Real-world use

A ladder against a wall, the diagonal of a screen, the distance between two map points — all are right-triangle problems.

⚠️

Watch the longest side

The hypotenuse must come out as the biggest number. If your "c" is smaller than a side, you've mixed up add and subtract.

Section 04 // Encrypted: GCSE Stretch

Advanced data

🔒 Rearranging, triples & surds
Finding a short side: rearrange a² + b² = c² to a² = c² − b², so a = √(c² − b²). Always subtract the smaller from the hypotenuse square.
Pythagorean triples are whole-number sets that fit exactly: 3, 4, 5 · 5, 12, 13 · 8, 15, 17 (and any multiples, like 6, 8, 10).
Surd answers: most triangles don't give whole numbers. Leave the answer exact as a surd, e.g. c = √20 = 2√5, or round to a sensible number of decimal places.

Distance between two points (x₁,y₁) and (x₂,y₂) uses Pythagoras: the horizontal gap and vertical gap are the two short sides, and the straight-line distance is the hypotenuse.
