Section 01 // Core Concept

The equation of a line

Any straight line can be written as y = mx + c. Just two numbers control it: m sets the slope and c sets where it starts.

y = mx + c

m — the gradient

How steep the line is. Bigger m = steeper. Positive m slopes uphill ↗; negative m slopes downhill ↘. Gradient = rise ÷ run.

c — the y-intercept

Where the line crosses the y-axis (when x = 0). Change c and the whole line slides straight up or down.

Section 03 // Read The Slope

What the gradient tells you

↗

Positive gradient

m > 0: the line goes up from left to right. The bigger m is, the steeper the climb.

↘

Negative gradient

m < 0: the line goes down from left to right, like y = −2x + 1.

➖

Zero gradient

m = 0: a flat, horizontal line, y = c. No rise at all.

∥

Parallel lines

Lines with the same gradient never meet — they're parallel, just shifted by different c values.

Section 04 // Encrypted: GCSE Stretch

Advanced data

🔒 Gradient from points & rearranging
Gradient from two points: m = (y₂ − y₁) ÷ (x₂ − x₁) — the change in y divided by the change in x.
Finding c: put a known point's x and y into y = mx + c and solve for c.
Rearranging: an equation like 2y = 4x + 6 becomes y = 2x + 3 — divide everything by 2 to read off m = 2, c = 3.

Special lines: y = c is a horizontal line; x = a is a vertical line (it has no defined gradient). Parallel lines share the same m.
